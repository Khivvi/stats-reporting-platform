Gender Wage Inequality in University Faculty Salaries

Ishani Bhat, Daniel Yan, Meg Kaki, Wonjoon Hwang, Raunak Khinvasara, Avanthikaa Srinivasan

Though a large raw salary gap appears in the data, after analysis the strongest evidence points towards structural inequality in rank promotions rather than systematic starting salary inequality.

Core Takeaway

Report-aligned
Men earn substantially more in the raw data, but much of this difference is linked to rank, experience, and promotion patterns rather than a broad within-rank pay penalty at the point of hire. There is however, strong evidence that men get promoted at a far higher rate when compared to women.

Why this study matters

In higher education, a raw salary gap alone does not prove discrimination. Salaries are shaped by rank, field, experience, degree type, and administrative responsibilities. This website separates three mechanisms: starting salaries, promotion to Full Professor, and the overall adjusted wage relationship.

1. Starting Salary

Do men and women receive different salaries at the point of hire after accounting for field, degree type, and experience?

2. Promotion

Are men more likely than women to progress from Associate Professor to Full Professor over the sample period?

3. Overall Compensation

After controlling for career-stage variables, does a broad gender-based salary penalty remain in the salary panel?

Methodology

The site summarizes the analysis choices from your report and shows the difference between a raw average difference and a difference that remains after adjustment for observed characteristics.

Longitudinal Panel

The same faculty members are observed across years, which makes it possible to study salary alongside rank progression and career stage.

Why Log Salary?

Salary is right-skewed, so log transformation makes model interpretation more stable and the outcome more symmetric.

Promotion Model

A logistic regression is used for the binary event of ever being observed as a Full Professor during the sample period.

Raw Difference

A simple average difference across all salary observations, without controlling for rank, field, or experience.

Adjusted Difference

A model-based estimate after controlling for observable factors like rank, experience, and administrative duties.

Structural Inequality

A pattern where gender differences emerge through promotion and representation in higher-paying roles rather than only direct pay-setting.

Limitations and Interpretation

Main Limitations

  • Single-university dataset, so external validity is limited.
  • Sample period is 1976–1995, so modern institutions may differ.
  • No direct productivity measures such as publications, grants, or outside offers.
  • Possible survivor bias because the data reflect faculty employed at the university in 1995.

Interpretation

The strongest conclusion is not that women were necessarily paid less than comparable men at hire. The stronger conclusion is that inequality in this dataset appears to operate mainly through promotion and rank attainment, which then feeds into the raw salary gap.
